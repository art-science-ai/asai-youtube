## Consideration Factors

### 1) Pain & comfort (Motor tunability + flange fit)
- **Why it matters:** Pain is primarily caused by excessive vacuum, improper cycle speed, or a flange that pinches/creates friction. A pump with fine control and correct flange sizing reduces pain and often improves milk output.
- **Good performance:** Independent control of cycle speed and vacuum; multiple flange sizes or a genuinely adaptive silicone cushion; quiet motor to allow gentle ramp-up.
- **Trade-offs:** Wearables often trade tunability and flange choices for mobility; budget pumps may have fewer granular controls.

### 2) Hygiene & maintenance (Closed system & spare parts)
- **Why it matters:** Closed systems prevent milk/backflow from reaching tubing and motors, reducing contamination risk and making cleaning/maintenance easier. Ready access to replacement valves and duckbills prevents loss of suction over time.
- **Good performance:** Explicit closed-system design; easy-to-buy valves/flanges in local market.
- **Trade-offs:** Closed systems are generally more desirable but check the model docs â€” many budget wearables use marketing language without detailed specs.

### 3) Convenience & portability (Wearable vs Desktop, noise, battery)
- **Why it matters:** Mobility decides whether your wife can pump during light chores or must sit still; noise level affects night pumping and ease of use around family.
- **Good performance:** Wearables for mobility; quiet desktop pumps with rechargeable batteries for night-time convenience.
- **Trade-offs:** Wearables trade off some suction power and flange options for hands-free convenience.
