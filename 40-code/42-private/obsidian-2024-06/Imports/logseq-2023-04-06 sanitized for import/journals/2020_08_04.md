- [[Freewrite]] on [[Film Noir]] from Dynalist on [[June 24th, 2020]]
	 - Thoughts on film noir. What are my thoughts on film noir? Let's start with what is film noir?

	 - What is film noir? Film noir is a genre of film that originated in the late 1930s / early 1940s in America. Roughly coinciding with the second world war. The term film noir was a later appellation applied by French film critics (hence the language of the term) who were deprived of American film during the war years, and when they started watching american films again in the post-war years, they suddenly noticed a marked shift in the style, tone, and thematic elements of the films, which led them to characterize it as film noir.

	 - What does this "noir" in film noir refer to? And what was the shift observed by the critics? We can break the genre down into two elements. One is visual style and the other is thematic. If we define the genre in terms of visual style, it is much broader. If we restrict the definition to the thematic elements the definition is more narrow.

	 - What are the visual elements of film noir? This is hard for me, because I'm not so good at observing the visual elements of film. For me, it's as difficult as observing smell and taste. But it is important. Visual darkness is one characteristic. Much of the film will be set in dark lighting. Usually this means that it will be restricted to indoor spaces or to outdoor spaces in the nighttime. Outdoor spaces in broad daylight are a small part of noir, we don't see it much. There's also a lot of play of shadows in the genre. This is super evident in the Third Man where we see the gigantic shadows play a big role. The shadow of the balloon salesman for example in a crucial scene overwhelms the scene. Third man is probably a very classic example of Noir as a visual style, almost archetypical, although it lacks many of the thematic elements of Noir (femme fatale, morally ambiguous / grey protagonist, etc.). What are the other visual elements? One is called chiaroscuro or something like htat. Which means that scenes are shot at odd angles. What about more prominent things? I think noir also involves a lot of feeling of being trapped and confined. The visual style must mimic the thematic sense of the protagonist feeling trapped. Think of Detour, where the guy is boxed in constantly in a car or in a small hotel room.

	 - Next are the thematic elements of noir. Here I'm more comfortable.
		 - Moral ambiguity of the protagonist. The protagonist is usually a grey character. He has one foot in normal american society, and another foot in something a little darker. It may be that they start out completely white, but soon start descending into the greyness as the film progresses. But certainly a big theme is the descent into the grey area. And the viewer finds himself sympathizing with the protagonist who has gone into the grey area. It makes us question why and how come we are rooting for this person. Classic examples are the insurance salesman Neff from Double Indemnity and Bogie from Maltese Falcon. They still have redeeming characteristics, but start descending into grey areas. Flirting with crime.

		 - Criminality. All noirs deal with criminality. The extent of the protagonist's involvement changes, but some crime is at the heart of all noir films. It may be a past crime (out of the past), a crime that recently happened (shadow of a doubt), a crime that one is only flirting with (maltese falcon), or a crime that is at the very center of the entire narrative (double indemnity), but crime is key to noir.

		 - Femme fatale. Not sure if this is called a theme or a trope, but in either case, it is one of the key elements of noir. Not as key as the above points, but quite key. There is a female character who appears to fit the "damsel in distress" stereotype, but ultimately turns out to be a fatal encounter. Often this character forms the bridge or the motivation for the protagonist to get into the world of criminality and greyness.

		 - Fate and the lack of agency. This is one of the more complex themes, but ever present nonetheless. The protagonist is seemingly wrapped up in events that he has no contorl over, or that are spinning out of his control, and he seems to be guided by forces larger than himself a la greek tragedy. This is quite explicit in some films like Detour but in other cases implicit (where?).

		 - Those are the themes I can think of so far.

	 - Is it just American? Yes, the classic film noir is american and it extends from late 30s to late 50s. Maybe 1940 - 1958 is the classic period. But then others started to experiment with this sytle as well. Japanese directors like Kurosawa. Then it was a huge inspiration for the French new wave. Many extended the concept broadly, and this led to the birth of neo-noir as a genre.

	 - One of the most fun things about noir is that it produced a bunch of great films, not just noir films. A tour of film noir is also a tour of great cinema. Filmmakers worked within the confines of this style / genre, but also transcended it and made great films. A film might only be tangentially a noir. For example, this is the case with many genres. Think about horror as a genre. Think even more narrowly about zombie movies as a sub-genre. It's possible to make great zombie movies that speak to ever present human themes.
