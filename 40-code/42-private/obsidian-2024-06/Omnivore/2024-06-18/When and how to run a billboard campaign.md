Every two years, our team at Stytch runs a large out-of-home (OOH) campaign in San Francisco‚Äîphysical or outdoor advertising such as billboards, bus ads, airport takeovers, or cyber-truck wraps. ‚ÄúIs it worth it?‚Äù is one of the most common questions I get from B2B SaaS founders and go-to-market leaders. 

It may seem counterintuitive to advertise modern software on a medium as old-school as a bus ad. But we‚Äôve found the real world to be one of the most effective channels to grow and establish our brand.

The value and strategy vary widely depending on the company or product profile, so to give you a holistic perspective on when and how to run a successful OOH campaign for a B2B software product, I asked leaders at Brex, Ramp, Notion, Vanta, Rippling, Canva, Writer, Bland, and Statsig about their lessons from running similar campaigns.

### **Evaluating ROI: How can I decide if it‚Äôs ‚Äúworth it‚Äù?**

When I get asked this, SaaS startup leaders and founders are often thinking about sales pipeline and revenue. These are important, but the ROI question for out-of-home is much more nuanced.

Stytch‚Äôs most recent OOH campaign ran for a month in San Francisco in April 2024\. For that campaign, we bought 80 bus ads, 130 bus and transit shelter ads, eight billboards on SF city streets, and, yes, three billboards on Highway 101\. We used those billboards to sell our B2B SaaS product‚Äîin our case, authentication APIs‚Äîbut also to communicate what Stytch is and establish our brand among the city‚Äôs high concentration of software engineers.

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sfZ74SyVkvuUEqBQKvZPyKe_ycWiEnfjt3ueDymsti2A/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1c3a4696-6c96-4fc3-9c8a-040a95856d7c_1600x1131.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/d32229a5-4c73-4e10-a06e-24530a13ebe6?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

And it worked! During the campaign, we saw a more than 25% uplift in branded search traffic and an over 70% increase in branded search clicks _globally_ (social media is powerful!). We also sustained a 10% to 15% uplift after the billboards came down. 

Further down the funnel, more than 10% of our sales opportunities created since the campaign have explicitly cited billboards or bus ads as a factor in exploring Stytch. And employees have posted hundreds of pictures to our internal #billboard-spotting Slack channel (on average, 2.1 per employee)‚Äîso it‚Äôs pretty good for team morale too!

It‚Äôs important to remember that billboards and other OOH spots are broad-stroke mediums, so they work best for high-level brand awareness, rather than immediate pipeline generation. For B2B businesses especially, the reality is that only a fraction of your ideal customer profile is actually in the market right now. An OOH campaign aims to make your audience feel something_now_ so that they remember your brand_in the future_. 

If the following traits match your B2B SaaS company, an out-of-home campaign is a no-brainer to my mind:

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sthpOt86gPUeTxZVAjNeRNie00F4E3bOeeV7lKRTNz-w/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2160,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F57232f1e-58d5-4a5f-ac56-f76b50dc8147_1080x597.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/a14a7f35-9092-4df9-b65b-df10ad56e2c3?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

Unlike with digital demand generation (think: Google Ads), where there are tracking pixels, UTM tags, and a clear view of money in versus money out, attribution for physical ads is murky and imperfect. You may never get a big final number to share in your board presentation, and that‚Äôs OK. 

Instead, brand awareness from OOH affects a range of metrics up and down the funnel, which combine to make the investment worth it. I heard from other startup leaders about the impact they‚Äôve seen from their campaigns:

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,s1pt547mBdrNHvBU9g2Luukd2d8LcHCHuLUjwDK_BxD8/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F13e5b52b-8c3f-4c90-ba94-f98879aa86fd_2516x2569.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/09c66d65-5251-4d01-80eb-cff16731426c?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

To quote Brandon Camhi from Rippling, ‚ÄúWith out-of-home advertising, you should make the investment because the logic makes sense. While you should try to get a directional read on OOH impact, you also have to use your best judgment around where your audience is and what message they respond to. Intuition can carry you far in marketing.‚Äù

And for Stytch, and almost every other company I spoke with, the logic is there when you want to grow brand awareness, you have the money to invest now to see impact later, and you believe you can create a compelling enough message.

### **Making an impression: How do I ensure my campaign is memorable?**

It‚Äôs important to acknowledge that the value of out-of-home is contingent on making a lasting impression. [Kira Klaas](https://substack.com/redirect/153671ac-7a15-4470-a6f6-c0d9db373520?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI) from Notion offered a great framework for how to think about what makes OOH advertising memorable: 

> _‚ÄúEffective OOH is about frequency (how often it‚Äôs seen) and resonance (‚Äòthis feels relevant to me‚Äô), both of which help with recall.‚Äù_

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sLp1qi3I7B45j5pMV_0QuXKn3AEJtK0rgyQmXHgZk6y4/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffd32eed1-36d1-4aed-8b2c-e40e3230a536_1600x707.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/3e1e49be-0e4a-4ba3-bd6d-11858151524b?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

For Michael Burke from Bland, the key to virality was sparking some controversy and then seeding virality with a clever video too:

> _‚ÄúTo go viral, I‚Äôve found that something has to be_ new _and_ slightly controversial_. Traditionally, billboards often have a phone number‚Äîreplacing it with an AI number is novel. Asking the question ‚ÄòStill hiring humans?‚Äô was concise yet slightly controversial. The billboard was inherently interesting, but our video is what went super-viral. This allowed us to have the billboard as the hook at the start of the video, the CTA at the end, and we could sandwich a demo in between.‚Äù_

And for Sarah Scharf from Vanta, the most essential elements were simplicity and humor:

> _‚ÄúThe most important thing is a simple and compelling creative concept‚Äîideally one that elicits an emotional response. It doesn‚Äôt have to be humor (as we typically lean into), but it has to be easily understood and memorable.‚Äù_

More likely, though, a single billboard won‚Äôt have a Vanta or a Bland outcome. And so a more immersive campaign, where users might have two to seven impressions in a city on a given day, is more likely to leave a lasting impact. 

In practice, what that means for us at Stytch is that we:

1. **Pick** **ad copy that will resonate** with our core demographic (software engineers)
2. **Aim for high frequency** within a relevant time and space (San Francisco, during peak developer conference season)
3. **Buy a mix of different ads**‚Äîbillboards, bus ads, bus shelters, newsstands‚Äîto maximize impressions
4. **Make OOH into a broader marketing moment**, with amplification efforts that span physical and digital, organic and paid

One of the brands I‚Äôve seen excel at having a holistic approach to OOH and online marketing is Notion. According to their former VP of brand and marketing, Kira Klaas, their success has a lot to do with taking full advantage of what a physical, in-person advertising format has to offer and leveraging it further on digital channels:

> _‚ÄúWe don‚Äôt just think of OOH as ads; we think of them as experiences. The more you integrate your OOH strategy with complementary channels, frequency and resonance go up‚Äîand the more effective each can be‚Ä¶_ 

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x550,sq0DTCi8_Jzs2Ftfr0aur1_vzbiU5rcv_1EujYakgxoU/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1920,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F20be0795-3af2-47cc-af50-ca3aaff42d11_960x960.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/cf5f80da-3b8e-477a-ad03-5e3d6ed6fd4c?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

> _‚Ä¶ As an example, Notion‚Äôs global pop-up series was a set of community-centered experiential marketing plays that focused on some of our top metropolitan cities. For each city, we chose a popular destination (e.g. where our target demographic lives or hangs out, where startups are based, which areas get high foot traffic) to host a Notion pop-up, orchestrating community meetups and driving both customers and prospects to stop by for a free treat and a chat with the team or an enthusiastic community member. Community leads from around the world organized their own complementary meetups and after-parties. We drove buzz on our own social channels and with partner influencers, and used limited swag giveaways to drive urgency‚Äîand, last but not least, we surrounded each city with OOH creative that created familiarity ahead of each activation and the feeling that ‚ÄòNotion is everywhere!‚Äô‚Äù_

At the very least, if you buy a billboard, share it on social media, a lot.

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sd25NxycBAue2VsRXSjn2-lqj-QrZlsSKowTmXRbwMyg/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2670,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffc883840-f565-4bf3-aaaf-8bfcb907306a_1335x1159.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/5dd83cf0-4b28-4ed0-91d3-c9ee58af1334?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

Cody Morgan, VP of demand gen at Ramp, offered this advice:

> _‚ÄúThe best tip I can give someone planning to invest in OOH for the first time is to get loud on social media when the campaign goes live. Hire a professional photographer to take photos of the placements to make it easy for team members, investors, and friends and family to post on social media.‚Äù_ 

Put all together, combining ad **frequency** and **resonance** leads to good **recall**‚Äîand nice boosts to metrics across the funnel.

### **Getting tactical: So how do I actually do this?**

At this point, we‚Äôve talked about the ‚Äúwhy‚Äù of out-of-home advertising. But there‚Äôs quite a bit of tactical work required, so I want to touch on that too. 

Here are a few grounding questions when you‚Äôre considering how to approach your campaign:

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sqK9ASMySee2pQ9KmXzU6eoNFuLSXjHIbGjFRHKDO5I0/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2160,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5774800f-28ac-425d-aafa-d1fa6d5e4f16_1080x905.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/e5ebefaa-d38d-4fd2-8c08-e28102d58d3a?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

#### **Right places**

For out-of-home, it‚Äôs not enough to know who your ideal buyer is; you have to understand their physical habits‚Äîwhere they live, where they work, and how they move back and forth.

For us at Stytch, we knew we wanted to target software engineers and tech workers, and we found that those people were heavily concentrated in San Francisco, or would at least visit for conferences. We mapped our target Sales accounts, typical commuting routes, and conference venues, then picked ad locations to maximize impressions near those locations.

[![](https://proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app/550x0,sR8yNUxuHJmNoDtKVkoOEPHHD3kdeHqvuSLXcoGSYgNg/https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3d380ed7-0046-4f2b-aa89-40e4bdaaa6ab_1600x827.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/371c9778-a94b-40bc-a9a0-8c9c1d681018?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI)

Lisa Ferragano, Canva‚Äôs Americas brand media lead, echoed this approach to targeting:

> _‚ÄúWe geo-target our OOH around regions with a high concentration of our target audiences, around specific events, and in downtown areas with a high concentration of large organizations that could benefit from utilizing Canva in the workplace._ 
> 
> _OOH allows us to reach decision makers in a context they might not expect, like on their commute or while they‚Äôre away from their desks. We‚Äôre taking B2B off LinkedIn and into the real world.‚Äù_

#### **Right place** _**ment**_**s**

Once you‚Äôve decided on a geographic location to target your ICP, you also need to think about individual placements. In out-of-home advertising parlance, that‚Äôs your ‚Äúmedia.‚Äù At Stytch, we were fortunate to learn from the best in [Kasper Koczab](https://substack.com/redirect/3f637c7c-8a38-4d7d-9c0c-7398188500f2?j=eyJ1Ijoibm10MnYifQ.NjEarT2ZIO18IdxC9k5a0j0BJcwjvF6cgtuRD-MCySI), who leads OOH at Brex. Here‚Äôs his advice:

**1\. Mix different types of ads to maximize both resonance and frequency.**

> _‚ÄúWhen entering a new market, we aim to have a good balance of high-profile units (usually units that people literally have to look up to see), along with a format that offers a wider footprint (street furniture or transit, for example) in order to deliver frequency. While folks might only recall the big unit on the sales call, the smaller formats that deliver frequency play a crucial role in driving recall.‚Äù_

**2\. Every placement is different. Consider the viewer experience for each ad.**

> _‚ÄúMarket rides with an ethnographic eye are central to designing a campaign that stands out. Is the highway littered with billboards one after the other to the point where no one is paying attention, not to mention remembering what they saw? Are the newsstands facing the street targeting the drivers, or are they better suited for the pedestrians who are two to four lanes on the other side of the street from the poster box? If the latter, then are my font, contrast, and graphics appropriate for easy legibility from the viewing distance?‚Äù_
